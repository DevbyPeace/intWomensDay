KEY MILESTONES IN WOMEN'S HISTORY

1848

Seneca Falls Convention The first women's rights convention in the U.S. was held in Seneca Falls, New York. Organized by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott, this event marked the beginning of the formal women‚Äôs suffrage movement. The Declaration of Sentiments, modeled after the Declaration of Independence, was drafted, demanding equal social, civil, and religious rights for women.

1869

First Woman Votes in the U.S. Wyoming became the first U.S. territory to grant women the right to vote. A year later, in 1870, Louisa Swain became the first woman to legally vote in a U.S. election. Wyoming‚Äôs progressive stance set a precedent for other states to follow.

1893

New Zealand Grants Women the Right to Vote New Zealand made history by becoming the first country to grant women the right to vote in national elections. This was largely due to the activism of Kate Sheppard, a leader in the suffrage movement. Her work inspired similar movements worldwide.

1896

First Women's Olympic Event For the first time in history, women competed in the Olympics during the 1896 Athens Games. While initially limited to a few sports like tennis and golf, this milestone paved the way for greater female participation in athletics.

1913

Women‚Äôs Suffrage March in Washington, D.C. On March 3, 1913, over 5,000 women marched down Pennsylvania Avenue in Washington, D.C., demanding the right to vote. Organized by Alice Paul and the National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA), this protest gained national attention and pressured the U.S. government to take women‚Äôs rights seriously.

1920

19th Amendment Passed (USA) After decades of activism, the 19th Amendment was ratified, granting women in the U.S. the right to vote. Susan B. Anthony, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, and other suffragists dedicated their lives to this fight, finally securing victory after years of protests, hunger strikes, and lobbying.

1928

Equal Voting Rights in the UK British women won full voting rights with the Representation of the People Act of 1928. Unlike the 1918 law that only allowed women over 30 with property to vote, this new law granted voting rights to all women over 21, making them equal to men in the electoral process.

1932

Amelia Earhart Flies Solo Across the Atlantic Aviation pioneer Amelia Earhart became the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic Ocean. Her flight from Newfoundland to Ireland took 14 hours and 56 minutes, proving that women could excel in aviation. She remains an icon of courage and ambition.

1955

Rosa Parks Sparks the Civil Rights Movement

On December 1, 1955, Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat to a white passenger on a bus in Montgomery, Alabama. Her arrest led to the Montgomery Bus Boycott, a pivotal event in the Civil Rights Movement. Parks‚Äô bravery became a symbol of resistance against racial and gender inequality.

1963

Equal Pay Act Passed (USA) The Equal Pay Act of 1963 was a landmark U.S. law that prohibited gender-based wage discrimination. It aimed to close the wage gap between men and women, ensuring that women were paid fairly for equal work.

1981

Sandra Day O‚ÄôConnor Becomes the First Female U.S. Supreme Court Justice President Ronald Reagan appointed Sandra Day O‚ÄôConnor as the first female justice on the U.S. Supreme Court. Her appointment shattered barriers in the legal profession, and she became a key figure in shaping American law.

1993

UN Declaration on the Elimination of Violence Against Women The United Nations General Assembly adopted this declaration, recognizing gender-based violence as a human rights violation. This was a major step toward legal protections for women worldwide, bringing attention to issues like domestic violence, sexual harassment, and human trafficking.

2005

Ellen Johnson Sirleaf Becomes Africa‚Äôs First Female Head of State Ellen Johnson Sirleaf was elected as President of Liberia, becoming Africa‚Äôs first female head of state. Her leadership in post-civil-war Liberia focused on rebuilding the nation, promoting women‚Äôs rights, and fighting corruption. She later won a Nobel Peace Prize for her contributions.

2018

Saudi Women Gain the Right to Drive Saudi Arabia lifted its long-standing ban on female drivers, marking a huge milestone for women's rights in the Middle East. This was part of broader reforms aimed at expanding women's roles in society and the workforce.

2021

Kamala Harris Becomes the First Female U.S. Vice President Kamala Harris made history as the first woman, first Black woman, and first South Asian woman to become Vice President of the United States. Her election inspired millions worldwide, proving that women belong in the highest levels of government.
